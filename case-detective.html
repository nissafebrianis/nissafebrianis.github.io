Case Study

Detective Mystery: Collaborative Online Game

A mystery game I led the design of to help a newly-formed team, scattered across locations during COVID, learn to actually talk to each other. Not through icebreakers. Through solving a kidnapping case together.

Format
Online, Game-Based
Participants
New team, 4 to 5 per group
Duration
Around 50 minutes
Tools
PowerPoint, Jamboard, Zoom
My role
Lead Designer and Facilitator
The situation

A new team that had never really worked together

This was an engagement training for a team that was brand new. They were working remotely from different locations, it was during COVID, and they had never properly met. The usual team-building formats, the ones where you introduce yourself and list three fun facts, were not going to cut it. People were already tired of those.

The real problem wasn't that they didn't like each other. It was that they had no reason to talk yet. They hadn't faced a shared problem together. So I designed one for them.

The team

Newly formed, spread across locations, working fully remotely during the pandemic.

The problem

Too awkward to communicate freely. No shared context. Needed to collaborate under real pressure.

The constraint

Had to work entirely online. No physical activities. The game itself had to create the urgency.

What we built

A missing person case with real clues, real suspects, and no single group that had the full picture

The scenario: Adinda Soraya, a member of a friend group on a trip to a villa, has gone missing. Participants are divided into four groups of four to five people. Each group is a team of investigators. Their job is to figure out what happened.

The twist was the one design decision that made the whole thing work: not every group received the same clues. Each group got five clues, but some were unique to their team. To reach the right conclusion, groups had to talk to each other, compare what they found, and piece the story together from incomplete information.

The cast

Everyone had a role, and not everyone was telling the truth

Each character in the case was scripted with a specific function: some were lying, some were telling partial truths, and some held clues that only made sense when combined with another group's information.

Missing person

Adinda Soraya (Dinda)

The person who disappears. Her social media, notes, and chat history form the backbone of the mystery. Groups need to reconstruct her state of mind from fragments.

Suspects

Rani Athyana and Ezra Rizky

Both had a motive. Rani and Ezra were secretly involved, and Dinda had found out. Their versions of events in the game contradict each other subtly enough that groups have to catch it.

Friends

Revan, Puspa, and Selly

Each holds a different piece of the truth. Revan and Puspa know Dinda wanted to escape. Selly, who is Ezra's girlfriend, reveals Dinda's less flattering traits without realising it helps the case.

Go-ship crew

Wayan and Suryo

Wayan handles the boat rental and can be interviewed about the logs. Suryo saw a woman in a black shirt boarding a boat heading back to the cave, a key clue that cuts both ways.

Witnesses

Pak Darma and Bu Leni

Pak Darma, the warung owner, saw Rani and Ezra together and also saw Dinda arrive. Bu Leni, the villa keeper, gives the opening context that starts the whole investigation.

Villa neighbours

Nadia and Deva

A couple staying at the same villa. Supporting witnesses who confirm presence and timing details that can corroborate or contradict other testimonies.

Session structure

Clues released in batches, pressure building as time runs out

The session was structured in rounds. Each round, groups received new clues and had a fixed window to discuss before the next batch dropped. This created the urgency that got people talking quickly, even the quiet ones.

Briefing
Setup

Case brief and group formation

Groups of four to five are formed and placed in separate breakout rooms. The brief is shared: Dinda is missing. The team needs to figure out what happened. Each group receives their first set of clues.

Round 1
First discussion

Five minutes with the opening clues

Groups start piecing together what they have. At this stage, everyone is still hesitant. The clues are deliberately incomplete — enough to raise questions but not enough to form conclusions. This is intentional: uncertainty is what makes people reach out to other groups.

Jamboard Initial hypotheses
Round 2
Clue drop and cross-group contact

New clues arrive, inter-group communication opens

The second batch of clues is released. Groups can now start reaching out to other teams to compare what they have. The escape room mechanic is introduced here: groups explore an interactive PowerPoint with clickable rooms, drawers, bags, and notebooks that reveal hidden information.

Clue Batch 2 Escape room (PowerPoint) Inter-group messaging
Round 3
Evidence examination

Dig into digital evidence

Groups work through Dinda's phone (password crackable from clues already found), her social media posts on Twitter and Facebook showing she wanted to escape, chat screenshots between suspects, and the suspicious boat log from go-ship driver Suryo.

Social media evidence Phone password Chat screenshots Boat log
Round 4
Final clues

All clues in hand. Time to commit to a theory.

The last batch drops. Groups consolidate everything they've gathered and from other teams. They form their conclusion: what happened to Dinda, who was responsible, and why. Each group documents their theory on Jamboard.

Debrief
Reveal and reflection

Groups present their conclusions, then the full story is revealed

Each group shares their reading of the case. This is where it gets interesting: groups who had different clue batches often reached very different conclusions from the same evidence. The debrief then opens into a conversation about how we collaborate when we don't all see the same things.

The clue system

Incomplete information by design

The most deliberate design decision was making sure no single group could solve the case alone. Here is how the clue system was structured and what each type of evidence was meant to do.

🗺

Character map with aliases

All groups

Every group received a map of the characters, but with fake names. This meant groups had to confirm with each other whether they were talking about the same person, which forced early cross-group communication even on something simple.

Purpose: creates a reason to talk immediately, even before the real investigation starts.
📱

Dinda's phone and notes

Partial per group

One group might find the phone password clue. Another might find the note that only makes sense once the phone is unlocked. Neither could read Dinda's messages without combining what they had. Her notes showed she was planning to leave her marriage, which changed how groups interpreted the "disappearance."

Purpose: one group has the key, another has the lock. They have to find each other.
💬

Suspect testimonies

Split across groups

Rani, Ezra, Revan, Puspa, and Selly each gave their version of day three. Some were lying, some were not. Ezra and Rani were deliberately inconsistent, but the inconsistency only becomes visible when groups compare their testimonies. No single group got both versions.

Purpose: contradictions only surface through collaboration.
⛵

Boat rental evidence

Key group only

Go-ship driver Suryo saw a woman in a black shirt boarding a boat heading back toward the cave. This is a red herring that also happens to be true, but in a different way than it first appears. Groups that had this clue without the social media evidence often drew the wrong conclusion entirely.

Purpose: shows that context changes meaning. The same clue means different things depending on what else you know.
Design decisions

Why it worked when conventional team-building hadn't

Shared stakes, not shared fun
Core principle
Most team activities ask people to enjoy something together. This asked them to figure something out together. There is a difference. Enjoyment is optional. Solving the case required actual cooperation, and people stepped up because the game created real consequences for not communicating.
Incomplete clue distribution
Structural choice
Each group having five clues, but not the same five, meant that reaching a conclusion required trusting and talking to strangers. You couldn't succeed by staying in your own group. The incompleteness wasn't a bug; it was the mechanism that forced connection.
Timed rounds
Pacing
Each round was five minutes. Short enough to create urgency, long enough to have a real conversation. The ticking clock cut through the awkwardness. When you have five minutes to discuss something with someone you have never met, you stop worrying about what impression you're making.
Non-competitive ending
Deliberate choice
There was no winning group. The debrief brought everyone back together to compare conclusions before the full story was revealed. This reframed the whole session: it was never a competition, it was a shared investigation. That shift changed how people reflected on the experience afterwards.
Rich fictional world
Engagement
The case had real social media posts, real-looking chat screenshots, a coded password notebook, and a clickable escape room. The detail mattered. When the evidence feels real, people treat the investigation seriously. And when they treat it seriously, the collaboration that follows is genuine.
What happened

From quiet to arguing about clues within twenty minutes

The shift in energy was the most telling result. At the start, people were polite and cautious. By the second round, groups were actively reaching out to each other, debating interpretations, and in a few cases, quite passionately disagreeing about what the evidence meant. That is exactly what we wanted.

01

Cross-group communication started naturally

Groups initiated contact with other teams without being told to. The incomplete clue system made it feel necessary, not forced.

02

Quieter members engaged

The game gave everyone something concrete to contribute. People who hadn't spoken much in the intro were actively sharing observations by round three.

03

Debrief conversations ran long

Groups had different theories and wanted to understand how others had read the same evidence differently. The debrief extended because the discussion was genuinely interesting to them.

What I learned from this

Game mechanics as collaboration infrastructure

The thing I found most interesting about this project was how quickly the game made the awkwardness irrelevant. People who were too shy to introduce themselves properly were thirty minutes later arguing about whether Rani was lying and what the boat log really proved. The fictional context gave them permission to engage fully, because nothing real was at stake.

The incomplete clue system was the decision I'm most proud of. It's such a small structural change, giving different groups different information, but it completely transformed what kind of communication happened. People weren't just talking to bond. They were talking because they needed to. That's a different kind of conversation, and it builds different kinds of familiarity.

If I ran this again, I'd build in one more debrief question: what did you assume about another group's information that turned out to be wrong? That moment of realising you made an assumption is where a lot of the real learning about collaboration lives.
