Detective Mystery

A clue-based simulation where players solve a fictional theft by analysing evidence, evaluating witness statements, and applying deductive reasoning under time pressure.

The context

A newly remote team needed more than a virtual coffee chat to build working relationships. They needed a reason to talk: a shared problem that couldn't be solved alone.

What stood out

Watching teams where one person tried to solve it alone: it never worked. The realisation that they needed others was more powerful than any instruction about "communication skills."

The problem

Remote onboarding for this team was efficient but sterile. People knew each other's names and roles but had no natural interaction points. Traditional team-building (icebreakers, trivia) felt forced and didn't surface the collaboration behaviours the team actually needed.

My approach

I designed a collaborative mystery game where each team member receives a different set of clues. No single person has enough information to solve the case: the only way to reach the answer is to talk, compare notes, and piece together the timeline. The game runs over a shared digital workspace (Miro) with a live facilitator monitoring progress.

The mystery itself is a missing-person case with a financial angle. Clues include bank statements, emails, timestamps, and witness statements, deliberately designed to reward different reading styles (some people spot numbers, others notice contradictions in narratives).

Design decisions

Incomplete by design. Each participant gets 3-4 clues, but the full picture needs all 12. No single person can solve it alone: this forces cross-group communication. No right answer revealed early. The facilitator doesn't confirm or deny until the group reaches consensus, maintaining tension. Built-in debrief. The last 15 minutes are a structured reflection on how the team communicated: who shared first, who connected dots, where assumptions got in the way.

What I learned

"The most interesting moment was watching teams where one person tried to solve it alone: it never worked, and the realisation that they needed others was more powerful than any instruction I could have given. The debrief revealed that the game taught more about team communication than a workshop on 'communication skills' ever did."
