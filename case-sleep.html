Case Study

Sleep Productivity

An e-learning module designed to help people build healthier sleep habits by working through the real obstacles that get in the way and giving them practical steps for each one.

The context

Most people know they should sleep better. Very few change how they do it.

The brief was not to explain what sleep is. People already know that. The harder part is protecting sleep when work, screens, stress, and daily routines keep pulling in the other direction.

I designed the module around three barriers that show up again and again: screen time, stress, and lifestyle choices. Each section moves from problem to practice. The loop keeps the module honest.

Learning design

How the learning loop works

Each challenge follows the same pattern: learn the core idea, complete a short check, and either move forward or review the material. Nobody advances until the understanding is there.

01
Start the section
Every challenge begins with a clear starting point.

Complete the micro-check
A short check confirms comprehension.
04
Pass or review
Advance if ready, or review the material again.
If not yet clear, review the material
If clear, continue to the next section

Read it left to right: the lesson introduces the idea, the check confirms understanding, and the branch decides whether the learner moves forward or revisits the material.

Core content

Three challenges, each with a practical path forward

The module is built around three real barriers to healthy sleep. Each one is grounded in research, explained through the audio-visual script, and followed by actions learners can try. Below is a summary of each challenge and the design approach behind it.

01

Screen time before bed

Blue light from phones and laptops mimics morning sunlight, signalling the brain to stay awake. Binge-watching is linked to lower sleep quality and more symptoms of insomnia. App design also makes it easy to keep scrolling, which is what makes this habit hard to break.

Practical guidance in the module

The National Sleep Foundation recommends stopping screen use at least one hour before bed. Night mode, keeping devices out of reach, and replacing scrolling with reading are the specific habits covered.

02

Stress and a busy lifestyle

When the body stays alert, deep sleep gets reduced. Night work and social commitments add to the strain, and stress can interrupt sleep rather than just delay it.

Practical guidance in the module

The content walks learners through setting boundaries with work, focusing on what is within their control, and building consistency in eating, sleeping, and physical activity patterns.

03

Unhealthy lifestyle choices

Junk food can disrupt sleep through refined sugars and excess calories. The brain's dopamine response to these foods makes sudden elimination unsustainable, so the module addresses the habit directly instead of pretending willpower alone is enough.

Practical guidance in the module

Gradual reduction rather than cold turkey, drinking more water, eating higher protein, and making junk food less accessible are the six specific approaches covered, each tied to the behaviour science behind the habit.

Interactive activity 1

Try it: how healthy are your sleep habits right now?

One of the activities asks learners to assess their current habits before receiving any recommendations. The point is to start with an honest baseline. Select the habits you currently practice.

Activity 1 · Self-assessment

Your Sleep Habits Checklist

Check all that apply

Be honest with yourself. Select every habit you consistently practice right now, not what you plan to do or occasionally manage.

✓
I stop using screens at least one hour before bed

Phone, laptop, and TV are off or away one hour before you plan to sleep.

✓
I go to bed and wake up at roughly the same time every day

Including weekends, because consistency is what trains your body clock.

✓
My bedroom is dark, quiet, and a comfortable temperature

Your sleep environment has a measurable effect on sleep quality.

✓
I avoid caffeine in the afternoon and evening

Caffeine has a half-life of about five to six hours in the body.

✓
I have a wind-down routine before bed

Something consistent such as reading, light stretching, or herbal tea that signals sleep is coming.

✓
I get 7 to 9 hours of sleep most nights

The recommended range for most adults to support optimal productivity.

✓
I avoid heavy meals or junk food close to bedtime

What you eat in the hours before sleep directly affects sleep quality.

✓
I do some form of physical activity during the day

Regular movement improves sleep quality, though intense exercise right before bed can have the opposite effect.

What I took away from this

Writing about habits without being preachy

The hardest part was not the research. It was writing it in a way that did not feel lecturing. Sleep advice can sound like a list of things people are doing wrong, so I wanted the script to feel more like a guide than a lecture.

The decision to address the science behind each challenge first was intentional. People are more likely to change a behaviour when they understand why it works the way it does, rather than just being told what to stop doing.

The scaffolded loop also pushed me to be precise about what each section was testing. You cannot write a meaningful micro-assessment unless you know exactly what the learning objective is. That clarity improved the content itself, not just the assessment.
