Sleep Productivity

A micro-simulation that teaches the compounding effect of sleep deprivation on cognitive performance. It exposes the hidden trade-off between short-term productivity and long-term cognitive performance.

The context

Sleep is the foundation of cognitive performance, yet it's the first thing people sacrifice when they feel busy. This module was designed for professionals who know sleep matters but struggle to protect it against daily demands.

The problem

The challenge isn't awareness: everyone knows they should sleep more. The challenge is competing priorities: work deadlines, social pressure, entertainment, and stress all pull against good sleep habits. Most sleep content is either too clinical (sleep hygiene checklists) or too vague ("prioritise rest"). Neither addresses the real daily friction.

What stood out

The notification audit worksheet was the most effective tool: people were genuinely surprised by how many times they checked their phone in the hour before bed.

My approach

The module tackles three specific barriers: screen time, stress, and lifestyle, each as its own section with practical tools:

Screen time covers blue light, notification habits, and the "just five more minutes" loop. Tools include a wind-down routine builder and a notification audit worksheet.

Stress covers the cortisol-sleep loop, racing thoughts at bedtime, and how to build a transition ritual. Tools include a brain-dump template and a breathing exercise guide.

Lifestyle covers caffeine timing, meal timing, exercise, and consistency. Tools include a personal sleep schedule calculator and a habit tracker template.

Design decisions

One barrier at a time. Each section is self-contained: users pick their biggest problem and start there. Tools over theory. Every section ends with a downloadable/printable tool. No perfectionism. The module explicitly tells users that "perfect sleep" isn't the goal: better sleep on your own terms is.

What I learned

"The notification audit worksheet was the most effective tool: people were genuinely surprised by how many times they checked their phone in the hour before bed. It's one thing to know screens are a problem, another to see the actual count."
