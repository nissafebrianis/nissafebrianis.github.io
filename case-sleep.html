Case Study

Sleep Productivity

An e-learning module designed to help people build healthier sleep habits — not by lecturing them about sleep, but by walking them through the real obstacles that get in the way and giving them practical tools to address each one.

The context

Most people know they should sleep better. Very few actually change how they do it.

The brief for this module wasn't to explain what sleep is, or even why it matters. People already know sleep is important. What they don't know, or haven't figured out, is how to actually protect it when everything else in their life is competing for the same hours.

So I designed the module around the three barriers that most consistently get in the way: screen time, stress, and lifestyle choices. For each one, the content moves from explaining the problem to giving learners something they can actually try. The learning loop structure kept every section accountable to that goal.

Learning design

The same loop runs through every section of the module

Each challenge follows a scaffolded cycle: learners read the theory, complete a micro-assessment, and either move forward or return to review. Nobody advances until they've shown they've understood. It sounds simple, but this structure changes the relationship between learning and testing entirely.

Core content

Three challenges, each with a practical path forward

The module is built around three real barriers to healthy sleep. Each one is grounded in research, explained through the audio-visual script, and followed by specific actions learners can take. Below is a summary of each challenge and the design approach for the content.

01

Screen time before bed

Blue light from phones and laptops mimics morning sunlight, signalling the brain to stay awake. Binge-watching is linked to lower sleep quality and more symptoms of insomnia. Smartphone apps are designed to keep people coming back — which is what makes this challenge genuinely hard to resist.

Practical guidance in the module

The National Sleep Foundation recommends stopping screen use at least one hour before bed. Night mode, keeping devices out of reach, and replacing scrolling with reading are the specific habits covered.

02

Stress and a busy lifestyle

When the body is under stress, it enters a state of alertness that actively reduces deep sleep. Nighttime work and social commitments compound this. The research shows that stress does not just delay sleep — it can create new sleep disruptions and worsen existing ones.

Practical guidance in the module

The content walks learners through setting boundaries with work, avoiding overthinking by focusing on what's within their control, and building consistency in eating, sleeping, and physical activity patterns.

03

Unhealthy lifestyle choices

Junk food disrupts sleep through refined sugars and excess calories. The brain's dopamine response to these foods makes sudden elimination unsustainable. The module acknowledges this directly rather than pretending willpower alone is enough.

Practical guidance in the module

Gradual reduction rather than cold turkey, drinking more water, eating higher protein, and making junk food less accessible are the six specific approaches covered, each tied to the behaviour science behind the habit.

Stress and sleep

What I took away from this

Writing about habits without being preachy

The hardest part of scripting this module wasn't finding the research. It was figuring out how to present it in a way that didn't make people feel lectured. Sleep advice tends to read like a list of things you're doing wrong. I worked hard to make the script feel more like a conversation with someone who understands that life is busy, screens are addictive by design, and junk food tastes good for biological reasons.

The decision to address the science behind each challenge first — why blue light is similar to sunlight, why junk food targets pleasure receptors, why stress triggers alertness — was intentional. People are more likely to change a behaviour when they understand why it works the way it does, rather than just being told what to stop doing.

The scaffolded loop structure also pushed me to be precise about what each section was actually testing. You can't write a meaningful micro-assessment unless you know exactly what the learning objective was. That accountability improved the content itself, not just the assessment.
