Efficient Budgeting

A financial simulation that helps users distinguish real needs from lifestyle wants, built around a single realisation: most budgeting advice assumes you already know the difference.

The context

Most personal finance education assumes that if people know how to budget, they will do it. But the gap between knowing and doing is where most financial plans fall apart. This module was designed to bridge that gap.

Quick summary

A self-paced simulation using three budgeting methods (50/30/20, envelope, zero-based). Users learn by doing, not by reading.

The problem

People understand budgeting in theory: track income, categorise expenses, save the rest. Yet spending habits stay the same. The real barriers are psychological: present bias ("I'll start next month"), mental accounting (treating different money differently), and lifestyle creep.

What stood out

The wants vs needs sorter was surprisingly polarising: people have very different ideas about what counts as a "need." That friction became the most valuable discussion point.

My approach

The module uses a scaffolded loop: learn, try, reflect, adjust. It opens with a wants vs needs sorter that surfaces the user's own mental categories. Then walks through three budgeting methods (50/30/20, envelope, and zero-based), each followed by a mini simulation. The final activity asks users to build a zero-based budget from scratch using realistic income/expense cards. Error states are designed as learning moments, not failures.

Design decisions

Practice before theory. The wants vs needs sorter comes before any explanation: users discover their own biases first. Realistic scenarios. All examples use typical Indonesian income and expense ranges. No judgement. The module avoids moralising about spending. The tone is practical: "here's what happens, here's a tool that helps."

What I learned

"The wants vs needs sorter was surprisingly polarising. People have very different ideas about what counts as a 'need.' That friction became the most valuable discussion point: it surfaced assumptions that a lecture never would have."
