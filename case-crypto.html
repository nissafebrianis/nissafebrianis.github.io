The context

This course was designed as a university elective for undergraduate students with no prior background in blockchain or cryptocurrency. The challenge was to build a curriculum that assumes nothing, but still reaches applied understanding by the end.

The topic is notoriously polarising: some students arrive excited, others sceptical. The curriculum had to work for both. I structured the sixteen sessions to move from first principles (what is a ledger, what is consensus) into applied discussion (use cases, risks, regulatory landscape) without ever assuming prior knowledge.

What stood out

The most effective design decision was the two-speed readings: every student could meet the material at their own level without feeling either lost or bored.

The problem

Most blockchain courses fall into one of two traps: they're either too technical (assuming programming knowledge) or too superficial (a high-level "what is Bitcoin" overview). Neither serves an undergraduate audience well.

Students needed to finish the course able to evaluate a blockchain use case critically: not just recite definitions. That meant designing activities that force application, not memorisation.

My approach

The course is structured in four modules of four sessions each:

Module 1: Foundations. Ledgers, distributed systems, cryptographic basics. Students build a mental model of what a blockchain actually is before hearing the word "Bitcoin."

Module 2: Cryptocurrencies. Bitcoin, Ethereum, consensus mechanisms, wallets, transactions. The focus is on understanding trade-offs, not just features.

Module 3: Applied contexts. DeFi, NFTs, smart contracts, DAOs, regulation. Each session pairs a technical primer with a case study and discussion prompt.

Module 4: Critical evaluation. Risk analysis, environmental impact, bubble vs substance. The final assessment asks students to evaluate a real-world blockchain proposal and recommend for or against.

Every session includes at least one interactive component: a debate, a case study analysis, a transaction simulation, or a group presentation. The goal is to keep students engaging with the material, not just listening to it.

Key design decisions

No code required. The course uses analogies, comparison tables, and worked examples instead of Solidity or smart contract code. Technical enough to be useful, accessible enough for non-CS students.

Two-speed readings. Each session offers an introductory reading (for students meeting the topic for the first time) and an advanced reading (for those who want depth). Students self-select their level each week.

Assessment as learning. The final project (evaluating a real blockchain proposal) is designed to mirror the kind of thinking students would do in a professional context. The rubric rewards reasoned judgement, not "right answers."

What I learned

"Teaching a topic where students range from true sceptics to early adopters forced me to design for both. The two-speed readings were the most effective decision: every student could meet the material at their own level and feel challenged. The final project evaluations were the most rewarding part: students who started the course unsure what a blockchain was finished it making reasoned, critical judgements about real-world proposals."
