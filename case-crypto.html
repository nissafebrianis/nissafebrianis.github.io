Blockchain and Cryptocurrency Fundamentals

A full university-level elective course I led the design of, working alongside an expat subject matter expert and a team. Built for undergraduate students with no prior crypto background, covering Bitcoin, Ethereum, DeFi, NFTs, and investment analysis across 16 sessions.

The context

Teaching a fast-moving topic to complete beginners

This was a group project where I led the instructional design process. We worked with an expat subject matter expert who brought current crypto knowledge, and my job was to turn that expertise into a structure undergraduate students could follow without getting lost.

The topic covers a lot of ground: Bitcoin's mechanics, Ethereum's smart contract ecosystem, DeFi protocols, NFT markets, macroeconomics, and the Indonesian regulatory context. Sequencing mattered a lot here. Each session had to prepare students for the next one, and the assessments had to show understanding rather than just recall.

Learning outcomes

Four outcomes that build toward application

I designed four course-level outcomes (CPMK) that move from comprehension to analysis and application. The goal was not just for students to know what crypto is, but to evaluate a project, understand the legal questions around it, and make a basic investment case.

1

Foundational understanding

A solid grasp of blockchain, Bitcoin, DeFi, Ethereum, smart contracts, tokenisation, and crypto assets, built to the level where students can support real business decisions.

2

Use cases and legal context

Knowledge of how cryptocurrency is used in practice, including the legal questions around ownership, storage, and digital identity in the Indonesian context.

3

Practical design and application

The ability to build use cases and concepts for blockchain integration across crypto assets, securities, NFTs, and future financial markets.

4

Investment analysis

The skills to assess a blockchain project, evaluate whether a crypto asset fits a particular use case, and analyse the fundamentals before making an investment decision.

Course structure

How the 16 sessions build on each other

Sequencing was one of the decisions I spent the most time on. I front-loaded the foundational concepts so that when students reached DeFi and NFTs later in the semester, they already had the vocabulary and mental models to engage with it properly. Putting everything on the table at once tends to produce surface-level understanding at best.

Sessions 1-3
Foundation

Bitcoin, decentralisation, and blockchain basics

The first three sessions start with the question most students ask first: what is Bitcoin and why does it exist, From there we move into how the network works, wallet setup, and what decentralisation means in practice, including the legal and privacy side.

Bitcoin history Satoshi Nakamoto Decentralisation Hot and cold wallets Blockchain mechanics Smart contract intro
Sessions 4-5
Context

Macroeconomics and blockchain scalability

Before going deeper into specific platforms, I wanted students to zoom out and understand how blockchain interacts with traditional economics, and why scalability is a real technical problem that shapes every design decision in the space.

Supply and demand Blockchain Trilemma Layer 0/1/2/3 Hard and soft forks Airdrops
Sessions 6-7
Platform depth

Ethereum, smart contracts, and on-chain practice

The most hands-on sessions in the first half. Students compare Ethereum with Bitcoin, explore smart contracts, and use Etherscan to analyse real wallet activity and mint their own token. Writing up the process forces them to explain what they did, not just follow instructions.

Ethereum vs Bitcoin Smart contracts PoW to PoS transition Etherscan analysis Token minting
Session 8
Midterm

Midterm: knowledge and application check

A formal midterm covering sessions 1 to 7, combining multiple choice and essay questions. Worth 25% of the final grade.

Sessions 9-15
Advanced topics

Tokenisation, NFTs, DeFi, and investment analysis

The second half moves into more complex and applied territory: how tokens and NFTs work in practice, the DeFi ecosystem, and how to evaluate a crypto project for investment viability in the Indonesian context. These sessions include group work, presentations, and individual analysis tasks.

Tokenisation NFTs DeFi Indonesia context Project research Fundamental analysis Investment evaluation
Session 16
Final exam

Final exam: comprehensive written assessment

Covers all material from sessions 9 to 15. Combines multiple choice and essay formats. Worth 30% of the final grade.

Assessment design

Checking understanding at every stage, not just the end

I used a layered approach: small checks after each session, practical tasks that require students to apply what they know, and formal exams at the midpoint and end. The goal was to make accountability part of the semester, not just something that showed up in the final exam.

30%

Final exam

Multiple choice and essay questions covering sessions 9 to 15. Tests both recall and the ability to analyse and evaluate projects and concepts.

25%

Midterm exam

Covers sessions 1 to 7. A formal checkpoint that confirms foundational knowledge before students move into the more advanced second half.

~22%

Practical tasks and reports

Two major practical sessions: using Etherscan to analyse real blockchain wallet activity, and minting a token on Ethereum. Both require a written report explaining what the student did and what they found.

~23%

Session mini quizzes

Five multiple-choice questions at the end of each foundational session. A deliberate retrieval practice decision, spacing recall across the semester rather than relying on exam cramming.

What I took away from this

What I took away from designing this

One of the genuine challenges with crypto as a subject is that it moves faster than you can write a curriculum. A session on "current DeFi protocols" can feel outdated within months. The way I handled this was to design around principles and mechanics rather than specific products, so that even when individual examples become less current, the underlying concepts still hold.

Putting macroeconomics in session 4, after blockchain basics but before Ethereum, turned out to be the right call. Students needed that context to understand why decentralised finance matters, not just how it works technically. That kind of sequencing decision is easy to get wrong if you design by topic list rather than by how understanding builds.

The practicals were the most satisfying part to design. Having students use Etherscan and mint their own token on Ethereum meant the theory was no longer abstract. They could see it running in real time. That connection between concept and action is something I try to build into every course I work on.
