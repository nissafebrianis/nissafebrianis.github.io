Case Study

Blockchain and Cryptocurrency Fundamentals

A full university-level course designed for undergraduate students with no prior knowledge of crypto — covering Bitcoin, Ethereum, DeFi, NFTs, and investment analysis across 16 structured sessions.

S1 Undergraduate
Sessions
16 sessions
Course type
Elective, Blended

My role
Instructional Designer
The context

Teaching a fast-moving topic to people starting from scratch

The brief was to design a full university-level elective course on cryptocurrency fundamentals for S1 students — many of whom had never owned or used crypto before. The subject matter is genuinely complex: Bitcoin's consensus mechanics, Ethereum's smart contract ecosystem, DeFi protocols, NFT markets, and how all of this fits into Indonesian regulatory and economic contexts.

The challenge wasn't just making it understandable. It was designing a progression that feels coherent, where each session gives students what they need for the next one, and where the assessments actually confirm understanding rather than just test recall.

Learning outcomes

Four outcomes, built to actually transfer

I designed four course-level outcomes (CPMK) that progress from comprehension through to analysis and application — so students don't just understand crypto, they can evaluate it.

1

Foundational understanding

Deep and comprehensive knowledge of blockchain, Bitcoin, DeFi, Ethereum, smart contracts, Metaverse, tokenisation, and crypto assets — enough to support business transformation decisions.

2

Use cases and legal context

Broad knowledge of cryptocurrency use cases, including legal questions around crypto assets, digital storage, and digital identity — applied to real project analysis.

3

Practical design and application

Ability to build use cases and concepts for blockchain integration across crypto assets, securities, NFTs, and the future of financial markets.

4

Investment analysis

Skills to assess blockchain projects, evaluate crypto asset suitability, and analyse fundamentals to determine whether a project is worth investing in.

Course structure

How the 16 sessions build on each other

Sequencing was one of the most important design decisions. I deliberately front-loaded the foundational concepts so that later sessions on DeFi and NFTs had something to build on — rather than introducing everything in parallel and overwhelming students.

Sessions 1–3
Foundation

Bitcoin, decentralisation, and blockchain basics

Students start with the question most people actually have: what is Bitcoin and why does it exist? These sessions cover the history, how the network works, wallet setup, and the mechanics of decentralisation — including its legal and privacy implications.

Bitcoin history Satoshi Nakamoto Decentralisation Hot and cold wallets Blockchain mechanics Smart contract intro
Sessions 4–5
Context

Macroeconomics and blockchain scalability

Before getting into specific platforms, students zoom out to understand how blockchain interacts with traditional economics, and why scalability is a genuine technical challenge — not just a buzzword.

Supply and demand Blockchain Trilemma Layer 0/1/2/3 Hard and soft forks Airdrops
Sessions 6–7
Platform depth

Ethereum, smart contracts, and on-chain practice

The most hands-on sessions of the first half. Students learn how Ethereum differs from Bitcoin, explore its smart contract capabilities, and then actually use Etherscan to analyse real wallet activity and mint their own token — reporting what they did step by step.

Ethereum vs Bitcoin Smart contracts PoW to PoS transition Etherscan analysis Token minting
Session 8
Midterm

Knowledge and application check

A formal midterm covering sessions 1–7, combining multiple choice and essay questions. Worth 25% of the final grade.

Sessions 9–15
Advanced topics

Tokenisation, NFTs, DeFi, and investment analysis

The second half of the course moves into the more complex and applied territory: how tokens and NFTs work in practice, the DeFi ecosystem, and how to evaluate a crypto project for investment viability — including the Indonesian regulatory context. These sessions include a mix of group work, presentations, and individual analysis tasks.

Tokenisation NFTs DeFi Indonesia context Project research Fundamental analysis Investment evaluation
Session 16
Final exam

Comprehensive written assessment

Covers all material from sessions 9–15. Combines multiple choice and essay formats. Worth 30% of the final grade.

Assessment design

Assessing understanding at every stage

I used a layered assessment approach — small checks after each session, practical tasks that require application, and formal exams at the midpoint and end. The goal was to avoid a situation where students could pass the final without actually understanding anything along the way.

30%

Final exam

Multiple choice and essay questions covering sessions 9 to 15. Tests both recall and the ability to analyse and evaluate projects and concepts.

25%

Midterm exam

Covers sessions 1 to 7. A formal checkpoint that confirms foundational knowledge before students move into the more advanced second half.

~22%

Practical tasks and reports

Two major practical sessions: using Etherscan to analyse real blockchain wallet activity, and minting a token on Ethereum. Both require a written report explaining what the student did and what they found.

~23%

Session mini quizzes

Five multiple-choice questions at the end of each foundational session. A deliberate retrieval practice decision — spacing recall across the semester rather than relying on exam cramming.

What I took away from this

Designing for a topic that moves faster than you can write

One of the genuine challenges with crypto as a subject is that the landscape shifts constantly. A session on "current DeFi protocols" can feel outdated within months. The way I approached this was to design around principles and mechanics rather than specific products — so that even if individual examples become less relevant, the underlying concepts still hold.

I also found the sequencing decisions more interesting than I expected. Putting macroeconomics in session 4 (after blockchain basics but before Ethereum) turned out to be the right call — students needed that context to understand why decentralised finance matters, not just how it works technically.

The practicals were the most satisfying part to design. Having students actually use Etherscan and mint their own token on Ethereum meant the theory wasn't just abstract — they could see it operating in real time. That connection between concept and action is something I try to build into every course I design.
